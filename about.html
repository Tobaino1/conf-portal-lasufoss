Context

We are very pleased to invite our colleagues from around the world to Lagos, Nigeria to the 2019 Annual Faculty of Social Sciences Conference Call for Papers and Panels , between 14 and 17 November 2019. Africa as a continent has witnessed series of positive developments and confronts many challenges in the 21st Century. While some of these challenges are explained as natural disasters, others are human made. However, the continent’s development paradoxes have attracted both endogenous and exogenous commentaries. Many have thus taken cognisance of the development paradox. The continent has numerous natural resources but this have not translated to massive human development. With over 1.2 billion people, Africa has a population capable of replicating the development experience of China and India but this has not (yet) happened. Bodies of water surround the continent but thousands still find it difficult to access drinkable water. The availability of fertile lands in many places has not been translated to food security. In other words, while coinages like ‘Africa Rising’ has somewhat replaced the ‘hopeless continent,’ there remains serious development challenges. These challenges include violent conflicts, the so-called ‘resource curse,’ malaria, unemployment, corruption, rising debt, environmental pollution and destructive practices, and growing population among others. The impacts of these insecurities affect a broad spectrum of actors that are not limited to African states and the international community. The 21st century nevertheless comes with a lot of allures and novelties, characterised by increased knowledge and information, development in science and technology, globalised economy and increased in migration, increase in population and urbanisation among others. The knowledge economy in this century is propelled by science and technology, a development that could be Janus-headed. On one hand this leads to human advancement and the creation of new kinds of jobs. On the other hand, massive technological leaps often create loss of jobs as, for instance, with the transition from coal to electricity. While the first and second economies of the world are already responding to this challenge and its(possible) future impact by strategic investments, Africa appears to be waiting in many instances. Yet, the threat is real for the continent and its people. For instance, many new and old oil-economies in Africa are suddenly confronted with the challenge of reduced revenue from crude oil export. In a world that is increasingly becoming aware of climate change and its impacts, these oil economies could soon realise that the downward trend in international oil price will become the new norm than an aberration! What happens when there are no viable alternatives to these economies? As a growing continent with abundant human and material resources that other economies crave, ironically these resources have not been properly harness by Africans to advance its development. Many countries remain rent seeking and their commanding heights are based on primary products. This is due partly to poor leadership, war and famine that have ravaged the continent.Vestiges of historical patterns and structures are still present. All these challenges are further deepened by the climate change that is capable of exacerbating conflict and poverty on the continent, if proper decisions and actions are not taken. Despite the challenges hindering development in Africa, yet Africans at home and in Diaspora are reputed for their contributions to science and knowledge production in the world. Africans have made positive impacts and innovative discoveries in science and technology, education and health, economy and all other human endeavour outside of their shores.The questions that quickly come to mind are: what hampers development on African shores? What is the place of Africa and African scholarship in the 21st century? Is the contemporary decolonisation debate relevant? Why has Africa not been able to harness her own resources for development? What options are available to address Africa’s many development challenges? Can the presence of China, India, and other emerging powers on the continent be sustained? What is wrong with leadership in Africa? How can Africa achieve economic and sustainable development in a globalised world?

Focus of the Conference

This conference invites scholars, activists, policy makers, and opinion leaders across the globe to interrogate the unfolding scenarios in African historiography and contemporary realities. Invitation is made for rigorous analyses and discussions around the various scenes and themes in Africa’s development. While we acknowledge the complexities of the continent and the position of Late Pius Adesanmi that Africa is not a country, Afrocentric counter narratives, and the futures that Africans and Africans in the Diaspora imagine and want for themselves are critical to analyses and discussions at this conference. Given the rising nationalism in the United States and Western Europe, the need to evolve Africa centre solutions towards the assemblage of new perspectives emerging from both African and Africana scholarship is pertinent at this time. Also lessons from non-African milieus are also useful.We invite interdisciplinary scholarship using empirical, theoretical and methodological approaches, including those highlighting African emancipatory traditions. The time is ripened only to critically engage dominant discourses on Africa but to also interrogate the inherited structures of knowledge production that (still) constrain African imaginations. The urgent task to critically think outside and within the box is therefore crucial for our discussions at this conference.
